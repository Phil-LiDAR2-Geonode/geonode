The Project

Launched on July 1, 2014, REMap is a directed research funded by DOST and is the 5th component of the Phil-LiDAR 2 Program.

REMap uses LiDAR and other geospatial technologies to assess the Biomass, Hydro, Solar, and Wind energy resource in the country.

It is a partnership between UP TCAGP and 14 other State Universities and Colleges (SUC) and Higher Education Institutions (HEI) across the country.

REMap's team is composed of individuals from diverse backgrounds -- Energy Engineering, Geodetic Engineering, Geography, and Geology among others.

REMap Silicon pyranometer installed at MSU-IIT.

REMap Components

BIOMASS

  • Considers four major crops (rice/palay, corn, sugarcane, coconut).
  • Computes for the theoretical biomass potential using administrative boundaries and statistical data.
  • Computes the energy content of biomass potential evaluated using theoretical potential, lower heating value, efficiency of collection, availability of biomass for energy production and area of region under consideration.
  • Produces resource maps based on agricultural maps processed and classified using LandSAT satellite images and LiDAR datasets.

HYDRO

  • Considers the head (defined by a head determination algorithm applied to digital elevation models) and discharge (determined using the Soil and Water Assessment Tool coupled to ArcGIS) in computing for the power.
  • Hydropower resource maps (run-of-river hydropower systems) are classified according to the horizontal distance connecting the virtual intake and virtual powerhouse determined using the head determination algorithm.

SOLAR

  • Computes National-scale Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) under clear-sky (cloudless) conditions.
  • Computes National-scale Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) under real-sky (cloudy) conditions for the development of large-scale solar farms.
  • Computes Urban-scale Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) on rooftops in selected urban areas under clear-sky and real-sky conditions.

WIND

  • Calculates wind power density and wind power using mean wind speed and wind direction generated from the Weather Research and Forecasting (WRF) model.
  • Wind speed and wind power density maps are classified according to potential turbine heights for development (at 80 meters and 100 meters) and to the years of simulation (2008, 2010, 2014, 2015, and 2016) to take into account climatic factors.
  • Micro-scale wind resource assessment for urban areas determined through the application of Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD).
